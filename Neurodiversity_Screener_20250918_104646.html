General Clinical Terms

TermDefinition
Neurodivergent Having a brain that functions differently from typical patterns. Includes autism, ADHD, dyslexia, and other neurological variations.
Ecological Model Understanding mental health through the interaction between individuals and their environments, rather than viewing differences as individual deficits.
Neurodiversity The natural variation in human brain function and behavior, viewed as a normal part of human diversity rather than as disorders to be fixed.
Person-Environment Mismatch When challenges arise from environments not accommodating individual needs, rather than from personal deficits.
Strengths-Based Approach Focusing on individual capabilities and resources rather than deficits, building on what works rather than fixing what's "wrong."
Masking/Camouflaging Hiding neurodivergent traits to appear neurotypical. Often exhausting and linked to burnout and mental health challenges.
Interoception The sense of the internal state of the body. Awareness of signals like hunger, thirst, heartbeat, and emotional sensations.
Monotropism Autistic cognitive style involving deep, narrow focus on specific interests. Can lead to flow states and expertise.

Limitation of Liability

Psychological assessments are tools that provide information about various aspects of functioning at a specific point in time. They have inherent limitations including:

  • Results reflect the individual's functioning during the assessment period only
  • Self-report measures depend on the individual's insight and honesty
  • Cultural and linguistic factors may influence results
  • No single test can capture the full complexity of human experience
  • Results should be interpreted by qualified professionals in context

These assessments are designed to inform clinical understanding and should not be used as the sole basis for diagnosis or major life decisions.
